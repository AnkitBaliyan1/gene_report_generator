Caffeine metabolism
Fast Likely to be a fast metabolizer of caffeine
Caffeine is a stimulant that favors alertness and briskness when consumed. The CYP1A2 (cytochrome enzyme P450 1A2) enzyme helps eliminate caffeine from the body. Changes in the CYP1A2 gene can impact caffeine clearance. For example, low CYP1A2 activity increases the risk of caffeine toxicity in the body; higher activity helps with better clearance and lower risk of caffeine-related side effects. If caffeine is present in the body for an unusually long time, it can cause nausea, acidity, anxiety, blood pressure rise, and palpitations.
Recommendations:
Watch your caffeine intake: Even though you likely metabolize caffeine quickly, limiting your coffee consumption to not more than 3-4 cups per day is encouraged.
Try other beverages: While you may not be able to start your morning without that cup of joe, you can swap your midday coffees with other beverages like herbal teas and turmeric milk, or even just water!

Caffeine sensitivity & smoking
Higher More likely to overconsume caffeine in response to smoking
Smoking speeds up caffeine metabolism. Rapid metabolizers of caffeine tend to consume more caffeinated foods and beverages. The nicotine in cigarettes increases the tendency to overconsume caffeine as it increases caffeine metabolism. Overconsumption of caffeine can increase the risk of high blood pressure and heart palpitations for a short while after consumption. Hence, avoiding smoking and excessive caffeine consumption is recommended to maintain good health.
Recommendations:
A combination of 2 confers risk: When coffee is consumed with cigarettes, coffee induces the blood flow, and cigarettes constrict blood vessels, which are at high risk of developing cardiovascular diseases.
Practice smoking abstinence progressively: Nicotine doubles the caffeine depletion rate. Hence, it makes a person overconsume caffeine at a faster rate of caffeine elimination. In the same way, caffeine consumption induces the habit of cigarette smoking.
Cut down caffeine gradually: Abruptly stopping caffeine intake can cause symptoms like headaches, tiredness, constipation, or even depression. Therefore, gradually reduce caffeine intake. You can start by consuming one less cup of coffee a day and see how it goes.
Be wary of passive smoking: Passive smoking (inhaling the smoke) may also increase caffeine consumption. Do not accompany your colleagues for their smoking breaks; while dining out, choose to be seated in a non-smoking area. Quitting smoking: Nicotine-replacement therapies like nicotine gums or nicotine patches are effective aids to help people quit smoking. However, these are to be used only after consulting a qualified medical practitioner.

Physical performance & caffeine
Higher Caffeine intake is likely to benefit your endurance potential
Caffeine, being a central nervous system stimulant, fights fatigue and drowsiness. This, in turn, can help boost exercise performance. Caffeine can help athletes train at greater power output and train longer. Depending on your caffeine metabolism rate, caffeine may favor endurance performance, power performance, or both. Caffeine may help boost sprinting/power exercises in fast metabolizers and endurance activities in slow metabolizers. Thus, the benefits of caffeine on physical performance are influenced by caffeine metabolizing genes.
Recommendations:
Check your caffeine intake: Limit your caffeine intake to one cup a day as overconsumption may have negative effects on your body.
Time your caffeine intake: Most scientific studies recommend drinking coffee one hour before physical activity to boost endurance. However, this can vary based on many factors.
Other ways of boosting endurance: If you are not a caffeine consumer, do not start consuming it only to boost your physical performance. Foods like quinoa, couscous, and sweet potatoes can help improve your endurance. Exercises like cycling, jumping jacks, stair climbing, and pushups can also help build endurance.

Caffeine-induced insomnia
Moderate Moderately likely to cause caffeine-induced insomnia
Caffeine is a central nervous system stimulant and can interfere with the natural sleep-wake cycle. It promotes sleeplessness through its interaction with a chemical called adenosine. The level of adenosine rises, slowing down all activities associated with wakefulness, thereby preparing our body for sleep. Caffeine's structure is similar to adenosine's; hence, the former inhibits the latter's effects. In addition, caffeine can also increase the release of certain chemicals like adrenaline, which further increases alertness. This can increase the risk of insomnia. The effect of caffeine on sleep varies among individuals due to certain genetic changes.
Recommendations:
Check your caffeine usage: It is recommended to avoid your caffeine consumption at least 6 to 8 hours before sleep.
Get moving to kick out caffeine: Minimal walking can tire you and exert a faster caffeine elimination rate than usual.
Melatonin knocks caffeine's sleeplessness: Melatonin can help counteract the effects of caffeine and promote sleep, but it can be impacted by the blue light emitted by phone screens. It's important to avoid mixing melatonin with caffeine as they have opposite effects. It's also recommended to consult a doctor before taking melatonin or other dietary supplements to improve sleep quality.
Enhance your sleep naturally: General aspects like regularizing your sleep time with a good sleeping environment, having a warm bath for relaxation, and massaging using essential oils like lavender can improve the quality.

Caffeine-induced anxiety & panic disorder
Lower Less likely to experience caffeine-induced anxiety
The "flight-or-fight" response is a physiological process that helps react to stress and threats. It is regulated by chemicals like dopamine, noradrenaline, and glutamate. These chemicals require adenosine for optimal function. When the fight-or-flight response goes into overdrive, it can result in anxiety or panic disorder. Instead of an actual threat, the body starts reacting to a "perceived threat" or an "idea of the threat." In some people, caffeine overconsumption can block adenosine activity, thereby resulting in errors in the flight-or-fight response. The risk of anxiety or panic attacks due to caffeine intake varies among people due to certain genetic differences.
Recommendations:
Adjust your caffeine intake: Even though you are a fast metabolizer of caffeine, if you experience any anxiety-related symptoms with caffeine consumption, lower your intake accordingly.
Avoid late caffeine intake: Caffeine might also interfere with sleep and worsen anxiety. Hence, consume your coffee early during the day time.
Diet additions: Include foods like fresh grapes, peanuts, peanut butter, pistachios, avocado, blueberries, chia seeds & flax seeds in your diet. They are filled with anxiety-relieving nutrients (like resveratrol, B vitamins, copper, magnesium, and essential fatty acids)

Caffeine-induced hypertension
Higher More likely to experience caffeine-induced hypertension
Blood flows through tubes called blood vessels. When it moves through the vessels, it exerts pressure on the inner walls, called blood pressure. In hypertension, the blood vessels constrict (become narrow); therefore, the blood flowing through them exerts extra pressure. Caffeine causes temporary constriction in blood vessels upon consumption. It triggers adrenaline release, which can cause blood vessels to narrow even further. This effect is temporary and lasts approximately 3 hours after consumption. Consuming 2-3 cups of coffee can increase blood pressure by up to 8 units. Certain genes highly influence the effect of caffeine on your blood pressure. Depending on the gene changes you carry, you may be more or less prone to caffeine-induced hypertension.
Recommendations:
Limit your caffeine intake: Consuming caffeine can cause a rise in your blood pressure by up to 5 to 10 points. Therefore, it's important to gradually decrease your caffeine intake until you no longer experience palpitations or abnormal blood pressure fluctuations.
Caffeine-free options for hypertension management: Try going half-caf or decaf to mitigate hypertension. Herbal teas like hibiscus and chamomile teas have excellent benefits for those at risk for hypertension.
Using apiaceous vegetables: Vegetables like carrot, celery, parsley, and parsnip can make caffeine stay in your body longer; hence, try avoiding the consumption of these vegetables for at least an hour after caffeine intake.
Manage your salt intake: Avoid consuming excess salt, as salt raises blood pressure.
Monitor your blood pressure: Consult a healthcare professional for a personalized plan like diet, exercise routine, and medication regimen to manage your blood pressure.

Caffeine & blood glucose
Lower Less likely to have caffeine-induced blood glucose rise after a meal
Insulin is an important hormone that regulates blood sugar levels in the body. Caffeine consumption can cause a spike in blood sugar levels in some people due to a hormonal response. Caffeine may lower insulin sensitivity and thus affect how the body responds to this hormone. This leads to decreased absorption and increased blood sugar levels after a meal. Another way that caffeine affects blood sugar levels is by stimulating adrenaline release. Adrenaline instructs the liver to release stored glucose to give a boost of energy; however, this can increase blood sugar levels. A person's genetic makeup influences how their blood sugar levels change upon caffeine intake after a meal.
Recommendations:
Watch your caffeine intake: Even though you are a fast metabolizer, try to avoid consuming caffeinated foods and beverages half an hour before/after your main meal.
Post-meal walks: Ensure you are adequately active for at least 20 minutes after your meal; this can help you maintain your blood sugar within normal limits by increasing insulin sensitivity.

Caffeine & heart health
Lower Less likely to have caffeine-induced heart health issues
A normal resting heart rate is 60 to 100 beats per minute. Heart palpitations are characterized by feelings of a fast-beating, fluttering, or pounding heart. Caffeine briefly increases heart rate and blood pressure upon consumption. However, caffeine doesn't affect everyone the same way. People sensitive to caffeine's effects may experience heart symptoms like palpitations. Further, caffeine acts on enzymes that stimulate heart contractions, leading it to contract with more force. Those with underlying heart conditions may be more prone to caffeine-induced effects on heart health. Therefore, it is important to consult your doctor on how much caffeine is safe for you.
Recommendations:
Regulate caffeine intake: Please check the caffeine metabolism section and follow the recommended level of intake.
Heart healthy practices: Incorporating meditation with deep and mindful breathing, walking, and other light exercises into your daily routine can help improve heart health.

Caffeine & appetite
Higher More likely to have caffeine-induced appetite suppression
Asprosin is a hormone that stimulates appetite by activating the brain's key "hunger" neurons, called AgRP neurons. A deficiency in this hormone can suppress your appetite. Caffeine affects the sympathetic nervous system, which plays a role in suppressing hunger, enhancing the feeling of fullness, and increasing fat breakdown for energy. It also acts on the asprosin hormone and reduces its levels, lowering our appetite perception. This is particularly seen in people who tend to overconsume caffeine. Having coffee right before a meal can lower your appetite and interfere with what you consume in a meal. Chronic appetite suppression can affect health negatively. Caffeine's effects on appetite differ based on consumption and certain genetic changes.
Recommendations:
Appetite suppression is not always good: Though caffeine-induced appetite suppression may help with weight loss, there are also severe side effects associated with extreme appetite suppression like headaches, nausea, sleeplessness, and nutrient deficiencies. If you do not consume enough calories for your body's needs, you may also experience more severe side effects.
Be wary of your caffeine intake: Consuming too much caffeine can reduce the levels of an appetite-stimulating hormone called asprosin. Hence, avoid caffeine consumption for at least an hour or two before your main meals.
Drink water or fresh juice: Drinking water and fresh juice can help you stay hydrated and might serve as an alternative to caffeine-containing beverages.
Avoid caffeine as a weight loss aid: Appetite suppression due to caffeine may make you consume fewer calories, aiding weight loss. However, this method may not be as effective as exercising or consuming nutritious meals.

Caffeine & iron absorption
Lower Less likely to have reduced iron absorption with caffeine intake
Iron deficiency is one of the most common mineral deficiencies in the world. Caffeine-containing products like tea and coffee harbor compounds called polyphenols that have an affinity towards iron. When caffeine is consumed along with a meal containing iron, the polyphenols latch on to iron, thereby decreasing the absorption of the latter. According to studies, drinking coffee and other caffeinated beverages with a meal is associated with a 39–90% reduction in iron absorption. The extent of reduction in iron absorption varies among individuals due to genetic differences in how fast the caffeine is metabolized.
Recommendations:
Limit your caffeine intake: Even though you are less likely to face iron absorption issues with caffeine, it is better not to have coffee with your meal. Some studies have shown that caffeine can interfere with iron absorption. Therefore, to avoid this, consume your coffee 1-2 hours before or after your meal.
Check your iron levels: If you experience symptoms of iron deficiency like extreme fatigue, pale skin, frequent headaches, cold hands, and feet, get your iron levels checked and consult a qualified medical practitioner for further course of action.

Caffeine & bone health
Lower Less likely to have bone health issues with caffeine intake
Calcium is an essential mineral for building strong bones and teeth. It helps to maintain bone density and strength, prevent osteoporosis, and reduce the risk of fractures. According to researchers, caffeine can interfere with the body’s calcium absorption, affecting bone health. Excess caffeine intake can result in reduced absorption of calcium in the intestine and increased elimination by the kidneys. Further, caffeine can also reduce inositol protein levels, which plays a key role in calcium metabolism. Lower calcium levels are a risk factor for many conditions, including osteoporosis and osteopenia. The CYP1A2 gene highly influences the effect of caffeine on calcium levels.
Recommendations:
Check your caffeine intake: Even though your risk for bone health issues with caffeine intake is low, it is recommended to avoid high caffeine intake as this can result in loss of calcium through urine. Limit your intake to one or two cups a day or per your recommendations from the "caffeine metabolism" section.
Support your bone health with diet and exercise: Calcium is the key nutrient for building bone, and vitamin D helps the gut absorption of calcium from the food we eat. Include foods like dairy products, sardines, green leafy vegetables, and tofu in your diet. Get enough sun exposure for some vitamin D. If required, visit your medical practitioner for supplements. Exercise is another key factor to support bone health. It helps prevent or slow bone loss and builds muscle mass.
